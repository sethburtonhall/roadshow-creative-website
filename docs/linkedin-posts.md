## Pillar 1 — Building Village in Public

---

**Post 1 — The founding frustration**

I built a product because I got annoyed.

I was helping organize something for a community I'm part of. Went to send the sign-up link and felt embarrassed before I even hit send.

Cluttered with ads. Confusing for anyone who hadn't used it before. Half the people gave up before finishing.

I thought: someone should fix this.

So I did.

Village is a group sign-up platform built for community organizers — clean, ad-free, and no account required for participants. Currently in early access.

If you organize anything for a community and you're tired of sending links you're not proud of, I'd love to show you what we built. → usevillage.app

#buildinpublic #saas

---

**Post 2 — A product decision**

The most important decision I made building Village wasn't technical.

It was this: participants should never have to create an account to sign up for an event.

Simple idea. Real implications.

It meant rethinking how we handle identity, email delivery, reminders, and check-in. More complexity on our end — but the right call for the person clicking the link on their phone while they're between meetings.

The best product decisions are usually the ones that trade your complexity for the user's simplicity.

What's a design decision you've made that looked simple from the outside but wasn't?

## Pillar 2 — Technical Takes

---

**Post 3 — A stack opinion**

I stopped recommending Next.js for every project.

For a while, it was my default answer. API routes, SSR, file-based routing — it covers a lot of ground.

But for marketing sites and portfolios? I reach for Astro now without hesitating.

Zero JS by default. Ship HTML. Add interactivity only where it actually makes sense. The performance difference is real, and clients never care about the framework — they care about the speed.

Save Next.js for what it's actually built for: dynamic apps with real data needs.

Right tool, right job.

#webdev #buildinpublic

---

**Post 4 — A dev philosophy take**

The most underrated quality in a developer isn't the code they write.

It's the questions they ask before writing any.

What problem are we actually solving? Is this the right abstraction? What breaks when this scales? Do we even need a database for this?

Most junior devs jump straight to implementation. Senior devs spend the first hour slowing things down — mapping out the decisions that are hard to undo.

That's the work. The code is just the output.

#webdev

## Pillar 3 — Agency + Engineering Observations

---

**Post 5 — Why agencies lose technical work**

Most agencies don't lose technical work because they lack developers.

They lose it because they lack a technical lead.

There's a difference. A developer executes. A technical lead owns.

Owns the architecture decisions. Owns the communication with the client when things get complex. Owns the call when two approaches both work but one is clearly better long-term.

When that person doesn't exist, the project either stalls, gets delivered late, or comes back with problems the client can't articulate but definitely feels.

I've spent years being that person — embedded in agency teams, handling the technical side so the agency can focus on the relationship.

If your agency keeps winning work you can't fully staff, that's the gap.

#agencylife

---

**Post 6 — Design and engineering stop talking**

There's a specific moment in every agency project where design and engineering stop talking.

You can feel it.

The handoff happens. Figma files land in a shared folder. The developer goes quiet. The designer starts asking "where are we?" The client senses something is off but can't name it.

What broke isn't the process. It's that no one was fluent in both languages.

Design and engineering aren't opposites — they're in conversation the whole time. Someone on the project needs to speak both. To translate a layout decision into a data model question. To push back on a design that's beautiful but will take three weeks to build and break on mobile.

That's what a technical lead actually does. Not just write the hard code. Bridge the gap.

#agencylife #webdev

**Post 8 — The founding problem**

There's a moment in agency work I kept seeing.

The brief lands. It's a good project — the kind of work an agency is proud to win. But it asks for real technical depth: multi-tenant data model, payment tiers, some kind of real-time component.

The design section of the proposal is sharp. The technical section is where things get quiet.
