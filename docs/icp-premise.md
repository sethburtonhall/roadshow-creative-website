# Premise — Ideal Customer Profile

## Relationship to Roadshow Creative

Premise shares the **same primary ICP as Roadshow Creative** — the design-forward agency without a technical lead — but catches them at a different moment in their workflow:

- **Premise** → the proposal stage (before they win the work)
- **Roadshow Creative** → the delivery stage (after they win the work)

This makes Premise a natural top-of-funnel for Roadshow Creative. The sequence: agency uses Premise to write credible scopes → wins more technically complex work → needs Seth to actually deliver it. Both products serve the same person. One wins the deal; the other builds it.

---

## Primary ICP: The Design Agency Proposal Writer

### Who They Are

An account director, studio founder, or senior project lead at a small design agency (5–20 people). They write client proposals regularly. When a prospect asks for an app with custom auth, payment tiers, or real-time features, they hit a wall — nobody on the team knows what to put in the technical section.

They're not developers. They're closers, creatives, and client managers. They're great at pitching vision and design quality. The technical scope is the part of the proposal they dread.

**Real-world context:**
- Boutique UX or brand agency that keeps getting asked to build web apps
- Creative studio that wins design work and wants to expand into product builds
- Digital agency where proposals are written by account or strategy leads, not engineers
- Same person as Roadshow Creative's primary ICP — just earlier in the process

---

### The Trigger

- An RFP lands that requires a detailed technical section — stack, timeline, team, costs
- A past project went sideways because the scope was wrong: wrong assumptions, wrong team, wrong budget
- They're competing against a more technically credible firm and need to match the proposal quality
- They've been guessing on technical sections for years and know it's only a matter of time before it causes a serious problem
- A client asks pointed questions about their tech recommendations and they can't answer confidently

---

### The Struggle

> "We're a design agency. Nobody here writes technical scopes."

**Specific frustrations:**

- Hiring a contractor to write a proper scope costs $500–2,000+ and takes 2–3 days — too slow and too expensive for every proposal
- Writing it themselves means guessing: wrong stack, wrong estimates, wrong team composition
- Guessing leads to signed contracts with bad assumptions baked in — scope creep, margin erosion, and client conflict
- Thin technical sections lose deals to competitors who look more credible and prepared
- The people writing proposals are account or strategy leads — not engineers — and they feel exposed when clients push back on the technical detail

---

### The Desired Outcome

A technical scope section they can drop straight into a proposal without editing — one that looks like it was written by a senior engineer, in under a minute.

**What winning looks like:**
- Respond to RFPs same-day with a complete, credible technical section
- Proposals that hold up under client scrutiny — stack choices with reasons, realistic timelines, honest risks
- Correct assumptions and exclusions upfront — protecting margins before the contract is signed
- Win more technically complex work without needing to hire a technical lead first
- Never feel exposed on the technical section of a proposal again

---

### How to Speak to Them

**Tone:** Practical, empathetic, no-nonsense. They're not looking for a sophisticated SaaS pitch — they're looking for relief. Speak to the specific dread of writing a technical scope, not to abstract "efficiency gains."

**Words and phrases that land:**
- "Proposal-ready in under a minute"
- "Written by AI, reviewed by you, trusted by clients"
- "Less than one hour of a contractor's time" ← already on the site, keep it
- "Stop guessing on the technical section"
- "Protect your margins before you sign"
- "The scope section that used to take days"
- "For agencies without a technical lead on staff"

**What NOT to say:**
- Anything that sounds like developer tooling or an engineering product
- "AI-powered platform" type language — too generic
- Features-first messaging — lead with the relief, not the output format

---

### Anti-ICP

Premise is NOT for:

- **Dev agencies and tech shops** — they already have engineers who write technical scopes
- **Solo freelancers** — they're not writing proposals in this format, and $29/mo hits different at that scale
- **Enterprise teams with internal tech leads** — they have this covered
- **Non-technical founders** — they're not writing proposals for clients; they need a builder, not a scoping tool
- **Agencies that don't write technical proposals** — pure brand/print/campaign studios rarely hit this pain

---

### The Funnel Connection

An agency that uses Premise regularly is pre-qualified for Roadshow Creative outreach. They've already demonstrated:
1. They take on technically complex client work
2. They don't have a technical lead in-house
3. They're willing to pay for tools that solve the technical gap

When the project is won and they need someone to actually build it — that's the Roadshow Creative conversation.
