# Village — Ideal Customer Profile

## Primary ICP: The Recurring Community Organizer

---

### Who They Are

A person who regularly organizes group events — not one-offs. They do this as a volunteer, as part of their job, or because they've become the de facto "organizer" for their community. They run the same types of events on a rolling basis: volunteer shifts, potlucks, practice schedules, class sign-ups, church service slots, neighborhood cleanups.

**Real-world titles / roles:**
- Church volunteer coordinator
- PTA leader or school club organizer
- Neighborhood association rep
- Youth sports team manager
- Community nonprofit staff member
- Office culture or events coordinator
- Sorority/fraternity events chair

**Context:** They're not event professionals. They're coordinators — people who took on the job because someone had to. They're time-poor, often unpaid, and just want the logistics handled without it becoming a project.

---

### Highest-Signal Sub-Segments

These three communities deserve particular focus. They all have recurring events, are deeply familiar with the pain of current tools, and have natural word-of-mouth density (i.e., one satisfied organizer tells ten others in the same network):

**Churches & Faith Organizations**
Running volunteer sign-ups, service time slots, small group events, and potlucks on a weekly cycle. Often have one dedicated coordinator managing sign-ups across multiple recurring events. High loyalty if the tool works — churches don't switch tools casually once they adopt something.

**Youth Sports Teams & Leagues**
Team managers, league coordinators, and club admins scheduling practices, games, and volunteer roles (snack duty, carpool, etc.). Event cadence is extremely high during season. Parents are the participants — they need a frictionless, mobile-friendly experience.

**Schools & Parent Groups**
PTAs, booster clubs, classroom parents, and school event coordinators. They run high-frequency, high-stakes events (fundraisers, field trips, spirit weeks) with large, mixed-tech-comfort participant bases. The "no account needed" feature is especially compelling here — not every parent has or wants another account.

These sub-segments are not separate ICPs. They're the most recognizable faces of the primary ICP — use them in marketing to make the message concrete.

---

### The Trigger

Something happens that makes them realize the tool they're using isn't good enough:

- They send a SignUpGenius link and a participant replies "I couldn't figure out how to sign up"
- They look at the sign-up page they just shared and feel embarrassed by the ads and the ugly UI
- A Facebook Event they organized got no traction because half the attendees don't use Facebook
- They spend 20 minutes trying to set up a sign-up form in Google Sheets and still aren't sure it works
- They get zero visibility into who signed up until they manually check

The trigger isn't always a crisis — sometimes it's just friction accumulating until someone finally searches "SignUpGenius alternative."

---

### The Struggle

> "I just need people to sign up for things. Why is this so hard?"

**Specific frustrations with current tools:**

- **SignUpGenius:** Cluttered with ads, confusing for participants, embarrassing to share publicly, slow to set up, dated UX. Participants often give up before finishing.
- **Facebook Events:** Requires a Facebook account. Half their community doesn't use it. Zero data ownership. Looks informal even when the event isn't.
- **Google Forms:** Works but feels hacky — no real "sign-up" UX, no automated reminders, no slot limits, nothing purpose-built.
- **Eventbrite:** Way overkill for free community events. Feels like it's built for concerts, not potlucks.

They're not looking for features — they're looking to stop cringing when they share the link.

---

### The Desired Outcome

A shareable link they're actually proud to send. Participants click it, immediately understand what to do, and sign up in under a minute — **no account needed**. The organizer gets notified in real time, knows exactly who's coming, and can reach everyone with one message.

**What winning looks like:**
- Set up a new event in 2 minutes
- Share a link that looks clean and professional
- Participants sign up without complaining or needing help
- Real-time visibility into who signed up
- Automatic reminders so they don't have to chase people

---

### How to Speak to Them

**Tone:** Warm, practical, slightly exasperated (because they feel you get it). Not corporate. Not startup-y. Like a friend who also organizes things and found something better.

**Words and phrases that land:**
- "No account needed for participants"
- "Clean, simple, shareable"
- "You're already doing this — it should just work"
- "No ads. No confusion."
- "Built for the people who run the thing, not the people who attend it"
- "Stop chasing sign-ups"
- "Real-time sign-up visibility"
- "Your community deserves better than a Google Form"

**Messaging hierarchy:**
1. Village is what SignUpGenius should have been
2. Participants don't need an account
3. Set up in minutes, not hours
4. You stay in control — real-time visibility, reminders, messaging

---

### Anti-ICP

Village is NOT for:

- **Ticketed event planners** — If money is changing hands, they need Eventbrite or a payment-enabled tool
- **Enterprise HR / L&D teams** — They have dedicated tooling (Workday, Greenhouse, etc.)
- **One-off organizers** — The free plan covers them, but they won't become loyal users or paying customers
- **Conference or large-scale event organizers** — They need complex registration, session tracking, and badging tools
- **People who need full CRM/attendee management** — That's a different product category

---

### Why This ICP Over the Other Seven

Village was built as a SignUpGenius alternative. That's the founding insight — and it's the sharpest competitive wedge.

SignUpGenius has an enormous, loyal-but-frustrated user base. They already understand the concept of group sign-ups. They don't need to be educated. They just need to see something clearly better and make the switch.

Going after "church volunteers who hate SignUpGenius" is more winnable than going after "anyone organizing anything." Start narrow. Once Village owns that niche, the other seven audiences will follow.

---

## Funnel Connection

Village has three distinct growth mechanisms — two external, one built into the product itself:

**1. SEO → Village**
Frustrated organizers actively search for alternatives. "SignUpGenius alternative," "free group sign-up tool," "sign-up sheet app" — these are high-intent searches from people who already know they need a solution. A dedicated landing page targeting these terms is the highest-leverage acquisition move Village can make. The ICP is already looking; they just need to find Village.

**2. Community word-of-mouth → Village**
The three highest-signal sub-segments (churches, schools, sports teams) are tight-knit networks with shared tooling. One church admin tells another at their regional conference. One PTA lead shares it in the district Facebook group. One sports league coordinator posts it in the coaches' group chat. A single satisfied organizer in one of these networks can unlock dozens of others. This is the compounding growth mechanic — prioritize getting Village into one node of each network.

**3. Participant → organizer loop (built into the product)**
Every time an organizer shares a Village event link, participants see the product in action. A clean, ad-free experience is the best marketing Village has. Adding a visible "Create your own free event" CTA on the public sign-up page turns every event share into a potential acquisition. This is the most scalable channel long-term — it grows automatically with usage.

**The sequence:**
SEO brings the first organizers in. Word-of-mouth spreads Village within their communities. The participant loop generates new organizers from the audience Village already reaches. Each mechanism feeds the next.
