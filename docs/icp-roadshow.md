# Roadshow Creative — Ideal Customer Profile

---

## Primary ICP: The Design-Forward Agency Without Technical Depth

### Who They Are

A small creative or digital agency (5–20 people) that wins product and web app work but doesn't have a senior technical lead in-house. They have strong designers, sharp account managers, and solid client relationships — but when a client wants something more complex than a marketing site, they need a technical partner they can trust to own it.

Seth steps in as their tech lead and lead developer: embedded in the team, owning architecture decisions, communicating directly with clients, and delivering production-quality code. The agency brings the relationship and the creative direction. Seth brings the engineering backbone.

**Real-world context:**
- Boutique branding or UX agency that keeps getting asked to build apps
- Digital product studio with design talent but no engineering backbone
- Full-service creative agency that wants to expand into SaaS and web product work
- **Live example: Matic Digital (Denver, CO)** — an existing Roadshow Creative client that fits this profile exactly

**What the engagement looks like:**
- Seth is embedded as tech lead / lead developer on client projects
- The agency manages the client relationship; Seth owns technical delivery
- Projects can range from product builds to ongoing development retainers
- Seth communicates at an executive level — clients never feel they're dealing with a contractor

---

### The Trigger

- They win a pitch for a web product or SaaS feature they can't fully staff
- A project goes sideways because there's no one to make the right technical calls
- They lose a deal because the client asked "who's your technical lead?" and the answer wasn't convincing
- They have a strong ongoing client relationship but keep having to turn down the next brief
- A past project delivered by a contractor didn't meet the bar — they need someone with higher ownership

---

### The Struggle

> "We keep winning work we can't fully staff — and we're tired of the scramble."

- No one internally to own architecture decisions or explain technical tradeoffs to clients
- Designers and engineers speaking different languages with no translator
- Junior or offshore contractors who need oversight the agency doesn't have capacity to provide
- Credibility gap when sophisticated clients probe the technical team
- Delivery feels unpredictable when technical leadership isn't consistent

---

### The Desired Outcome

A trusted technical partner they can bring into pitches and projects with confidence. Someone who makes the agency look bigger and more capable — handles the engineering, communicates well with clients, and makes delivery feel smooth every time.

**What winning looks like:**
- A go-to technical partner for every product engagement — no scrambling to staff up
- Clients who trust the agency's technical recommendations because Seth backs them up
- Projects delivered on time, to a high standard, without drama
- The agency can pursue bigger, more complex briefs knowing the technical side is covered
- A long-term retainer relationship that compounds over time

**Why this client pays well:** Agencies bill Seth's work at a markup to their client. High leverage: one strong agency relationship generates multiple projects. The incentive structure naturally rewards quality — if Seth makes the agency look good, the relationship grows.

---

### How to Speak to Them

**Tone:** Collaborative, low-ego, agency-fluent. They want a partner who makes them look good, not a prima donna engineer who makes everything about the tech.

**Words and phrases that land:**
- "Your embedded technical lead"
- "I've worked inside agencies — I know how to operate in that environment"
- "You own the client relationship; I own the technical delivery"
- "I translate between design and engineering"
- "Make your agency capable of taking on bigger product work"
- "One less thing to scramble for"

**What NOT to say:**
- Anything that sounds like a freelancer pitching individual services
- Technology laundry lists without context
- Anything implying the agency would need to manage or babysit

---

### Anti-ICP

- **Large enterprises** wanting staff aug — they want bodies, not ownership
- **Agencies looking for cheap subcontractors** — low-margin, high-friction, undervalues the work
- **Agencies that want to micromanage the stack** — if they already know exactly what to build and how, the partnership dynamic won't work
- **One-project shops** — the value of this ICP compounds over time; one-off engagements don't build the relationship

## Secondary ICP: The Funded Technical Founder

### Who They Are

A solo founder or co-founder who has validated an idea and secured early funding — angel round, pre-seed, or seed. They have a clear product vision and a working MVP (or a very clear spec), but they don't have the technical team to build it to production standard. They're not looking for an agency. They're looking for someone who codes like a senior engineer and thinks like a co-founder.

**Real-world context:**
- Non-technical founder who can describe the product clearly but can't build it
- Semi-technical founder who knows what they want but can't own the full stack
- Raised $150K–$2M and needs to ship before the runway runs out
- Has promised an investor or early customer a demo / beta / launch date
- Has probably already had one bad experience with a freelancer or agency

**Industries they tend to come from:** B2B SaaS, creator tools, marketplace products, community platforms, internal tools.

---

### The Trigger

The clock starts running when they:

- Close a funding round and suddenly have money but no team
- Make a commitment to an investor that requires a working product by a specific date
- Try to hire a full-time CTO and realize they can't afford one yet (or can't find the right person)
- Get burned by an offshore agency that delivered something unshippable
- Find a freelancer who's good at one thing but can't own the full product

At that point, they start looking for something in between: **not a staff aug contractor, not an agency — someone who can own it**.

---

### The Struggle

> "I need someone who can make the right calls, not just execute tickets."

- Agencies over-engineer, over-charge, and under-care about the actual product
- Freelancers are fine for tasks but need constant direction — they don't think ahead
- Junior developers can write code but can't own architecture or make product tradeoffs
- It's nearly impossible to find one person who does design quality + full-stack engineering + product thinking
- Every week without a shipped product is burn rate and credibility lost

---

### The Desired Outcome

A production-ready product they're proud to demo — built by someone they trusted to make the right technical decisions from the start.

**What winning looks like:**
- Concept → production in weeks, not months
- A codebase they can hand off to a future hire without embarrassment
- Auth, billing, onboarding, the full stack — handled
- They can focus on users and fundraising while Seth handles engineering
- A technical partner who communicates clearly and doesn't disappear

**Budget reality:** $10K–$50K+ for a project engagement. Monthly retainer ($3K–$8K/mo) for ongoing product work.

---

### How to Speak to Them

**Tone:** Direct, confident, peer-to-peer. They're busy, stressed, and BS-sensitive. Don't oversell. Don't pad. Show competence through specificity.

**Words and phrases that land:**
- "Concept to production"
- "I'll own the technical decisions"
- "You stay focused on the product — I'll handle the build"
- "Built to last, not just to ship"
- "Technical co-founder energy"
- "Full-stack from architecture to deployment"

**What NOT to say:**
- Generic agency language ("we deliver quality solutions")
- Laundry lists of technologies without context
- Anything that implies they need to project-manage you

---

### Anti-ICP

- **Non-technical founders with no funding and no budget** — the vision is great, but there's no engagement without budget to match
- **Clients who want to micromanage the stack** — if they already know exactly what to build and how, they need a contractor, not a partner
- **Budget-first buyers** — if the first question is "how cheap can you go," the fit isn't there
